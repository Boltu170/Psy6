Basic Assumptions & The Nervous System

Basic Assumption: All behaviour, thoughts and emotions have a physical/biological basis ‚Äî they can be explained by brain activity, genetics and neurochemistry.
Central Nervous System (CNS)
Brain + Spinal Cord
Controls and processes all information
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
Carries messages to and from the CNS
Somatic (voluntary) + Autonomic (involuntary)
Sympathetic NS
Arouses body to expend energy
Fight or flight response
Parasympathetic NS
Calms body to conserve energy
Rest and digest response

The Autonomic Nervous System has two divisions that oppose each other:

‚Ä¢ Sympathetic: dilates pupils, increases heart rate, relaxes airways, inhibits digestion ‚Äî prepares the body for action

‚Ä¢ Parasympathetic: constricts pupils, slows heartbeat, stimulates digestion ‚Äî restores the body to a resting state


The Somatic Nervous System controls voluntary muscles and transmits sensory information to the CNS ‚Äî it is under conscious control.


The spinal cord acts as the highway connecting the brain to the rest of the body, allowing rapid reflex actions even before the brain can process the information.

Frontal Lobe ‚Äî Motor control, problem solving, speech production (Broca's area)Damage = difficulty speaking or planning

Cerebral Cortex: The largest part of the brain ‚Äî 83% of its total mass. Controls all higher mental functions including conscious thoughts, experiences and intellectual functions.


Localisation of Function ‚Äî Language:

‚Ä¢ Broca's Area (1861): Located in the frontal lobe. Damage causes motor aphasia ‚Äî the patient can understand language but cannot speak in full sentences or express ideas in writing.

‚Ä¢ Wernicke's Area (1876): Located in the temporal lobe. Damage causes sensory aphasia ‚Äî the patient can speak but cannot understand language.


Hemispheric Lateralization:

‚Ä¢ Left hemisphere (dominant): reading, writing, mathematics, logic, speech, decision-making

‚Ä¢ Right hemisphere: facial recognition, voice inflections, visual/spatial reasoning, emotion, artistic skills

‚Ä¢ Each hemisphere controls the opposite side of the body (contralateral control)


Key brain structures: Telencephalon (cerebral hemispheres) ‚Üí Diencephalon (thalamus, hypothalamus) ‚Üí Mesencephalon (midbrain) ‚Üí Pons ‚Üí Cerebellum ‚Üí Medulla Oblongata

Psychodynamic Approach

Basic Assumptions ‚Äî Freud & the Unconscious

Basic Assumption: Much of human behaviour is driven by unconscious forces ‚Äî thoughts, desires and memories that are repressed because they are disturbing or threatening.
Conscious Thoughts
Wishes and desires we are aware of or can recall at any moment
Unconscious Forces
Repressed desires we cannot voluntarily access ‚Äî yet they drive our behaviour

Three Methods to Access the Unconscious

Free Association: Client talks freely about whatever enters their mind ‚Äî uncensored speech reveals unconscious material

Dream Interpretation: Dreams have manifest content (the obvious story) and latent content (the hidden symbolic meaning)

Freudian Slips: Mistakes in everyday speech that reveal unconscious thoughts or wishes ‚Äî e.g. calling someone the wrong name

Unconscious Motivation (Freud): The influence of repressed thoughts, desires or impulses on our conscious thoughts and behaviours. We are not aware of these influences.


Free Association in practice: Freud would ask clients to say whatever came into their heads without filtering. Hesitations, emotional reactions and unexpected connections provided clues to unconscious conflicts.


Dream Interpretation: Freud believed the unconscious mind is less guarded during sleep. Dreams disguise threatening material through symbols. The analyst's job is to decode the latent content from the manifest content.


Freudian Slips (Parapraxis): These are not random errors ‚Äî Freud argued they reveal what we really think or want but cannot say consciously. Example: A speaker at an event says "I declare this session closed" instead of "open."

Id, Ego and Superego

Freud's personality structure = a constant battle between three competing forces
The ID
Primitive, instinctual
Drives: sex & aggression
Follows: pleasure principle
Satisfy desires immediately, regardless of consequences
The EGO
Rational, realistic
Mediates between id and superego
Follows: reality principle
Find socially acceptable ways to satisfy the id
The SUPEREGO
Moral conscience
Applies parental and societal values
Follows: perfection principle
Idealise and civilise our thoughts and behaviour

The Iceberg Analogy

Think of an iceberg: the conscious mind (above water) = ego surface. The preconscious (just below) = superego. The vast unconscious (deep below) = the id and repressed material.

Development timeline:

‚Ä¢ The id is present from birth ‚Äî it is entirely unconscious and demands immediate gratification

‚Ä¢ The ego develops from the id during infancy ‚Äî it learns to delay gratification and find realistic solutions

‚Ä¢ The superego develops from the ego during early childhood ‚Äî it internalises the moral rules of parents and society


When conflict occurs between the id and superego, the ego experiences anxiety. To manage this anxiety, the ego uses defence mechanisms ‚Äî unconscious strategies to protect the self. Examples:

‚Ä¢ Repression: Pushing threatening thoughts into the unconscious

‚Ä¢ Projection: Attributing your own unacceptable feelings to others

‚Ä¢ Sublimation: Channelling unacceptable urges into socially acceptable activities (e.g. sport)

‚Ä¢ Denial: Refusing to accept a threatening reality

The 5 Psychosexual Stages ‚Äî Part 1

Freud believed personality is fixed in the first few years of life. Unresolved conflicts at any stage cause fixation ‚Äî lasting effects on adult personality.
Oral Stage (0‚Äì18 months) ‚Äî Pleasure centred on the mouth: sucking, biting, breastfeeding
Fixation ‚Üí oral personality: smoking, nail-biting, over-eating, especially under stress
Anal Stage (18‚Äì36 months) ‚Äî Pleasure focused on bowel/bladder control; first conflict with authority (potty training)
Anal-retentive (harsh training) ‚Üí obsessively tidy, punctual, controlling | Anal-expulsive (lenient training) ‚Üí messy, disorganised, rebellious
Phallic Stage (3‚Äì6 years) ‚Äî Pleasure centred on genitals; child becomes aware of anatomical sex differences
Oedipus complex (boys) ‚Äî castration anxiety resolved by identifying with father | Electra complex (girls) ‚Äî penis envy resolved by identifying with mother

The Oedipus Complex (Phallic Stage ‚Äî Boys):

A young boy develops unconscious sexual feelings towards his mother and views his father as a rival. He fears his father will castrate him (castration anxiety). To resolve this, he represses his desire for his mother and identifies with his father ‚Äî adopting masculine behaviours and values.

Evidence: Freud's Little Hans case study (1909)


The Electra Complex (Phallic Stage ‚Äî Girls):

A young girl develops feelings for her father and views her mother as a rival. She experiences penis envy upon realising she does not have a penis. She resolves this by repressing her desire for her father and identifying with her mother ‚Äî adopting feminine characteristics. Freud acknowledged this was less well-defined than the Oedipus complex.


Identification: The process by which children internalise the characteristics and values of the same-sex parent ‚Äî this is how gender identity and the superego develop.

Source: ARU College ‚Äî Psychodynamic Approach, Week 6 Lecture

Research Methods ‚Äî Psychodynamic Approach

Case Studies: e.g. Anna O (Bertha Pappenheim) ‚Äî diagnosed with hysteria by Breuer and Freud; became the basis for psychoanalytic therapy

Observations: Detailed clinical observations of patients during therapy sessions

Correlations: Identifying relationships between early childhood experiences and adult personality traits

Little Hans (1909): Case study used by Freud as evidence for the Oedipus complex

Anna O (Bertha Pappenheim): One of the most famous cases in the history of psychology. She presented with symptoms of hysteria ‚Äî a condition where patients exhibit physical symptoms with no clear physical cause, believed to stem from psychological conflict. Her treatment with Breuer (and later Freud) helped develop the technique of the "talking cure" ‚Äî the foundation of psychoanalysis.

Research Methods & Evaluation

üß† Biological ‚Äî Research Methods

‚Ä¢ Animal studies ‚Äî manipulate brain chemistry or structure

‚Ä¢ Case studies ‚Äî e.g. Phineas Gage (frontal lobe damage)

‚Ä¢ Drug therapies ‚Äî test effects of changing neurochemistry

‚Ä¢ Brain scans: PET, EEG, MEG, MRI, fMRI

‚Ä¢ Twin/family studies ‚Äî separate nature from nurture

üõãÔ∏è Psychodynamic ‚Äî Research Methods

‚Ä¢ Case studies ‚Äî Anna O, Little Hans, Dora

‚Ä¢ Observations ‚Äî clinical sessions with patients

‚Ä¢ Correlations ‚Äî childhood experience and adult outcomes

‚Ä¢ Dream analysis ‚Äî interpreting latent content

‚Ä¢ Free association ‚Äî uncovering repressed material

Biological Approach ‚Äî Evaluation:

‚úÖ Strength: Scientific and objective ‚Äî brain scans provide measurable, replicable evidence

‚úÖ Strength: Practical applications ‚Äî drug therapies for depression, anxiety and schizophrenia

‚ùå Weakness: Reductionist ‚Äî reduces complex behaviour to biology; ignores social and cultural factors

‚ùå Weakness: Ethical concerns ‚Äî animal studies cause suffering; brain surgery is irreversible


Psychodynamic Approach ‚Äî Evaluation:

‚úÖ Strength: Influential ‚Äî pioneered talking therapies; transformed clinical psychology

‚úÖ Strength: Recognises the role of childhood in shaping adult personality

‚ùå Weakness: Unfalsifiable ‚Äî unconscious processes cannot be scientifically tested or disproved

‚ùå Weakness: Based on unrepresentative samples ‚Äî mostly white, middle-class Viennese patients; limited generalisability

‚ùå Weakness: Gender bias ‚Äî the Electra complex has been widely criticised as inadequate and sexist

Source: ARU College ‚Äî Week 6 Lecture | Spielman et al. (2020) Psychology 2e
